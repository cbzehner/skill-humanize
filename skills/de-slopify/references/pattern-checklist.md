## Filler Phrases (delete entirely)

- "It's worth noting that..." / "It's important to note that..."
- "Let's dive into..." / "Let's explore..." / "Let's unpack that"
- "Here's the thing..." / "Here's why it matters..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..." / "In today's digital landscape..."
- "Without further ado..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "It goes without saying..."
- "When it comes to..."
- "In the realm of..."

## Structural Patterns

- **"Not just X, it's Y"** — The most overused LLM rhetorical pattern. Rewrite as a direct statement.
- **Em dash overload** — One or two per page is fine. Five per paragraph is a tell. Replace with: comma, semicolon, period, parenthetical. Vary the fix.
- **Transition word chains** — "However... Furthermore... Additionally... Moreover..." at paragraph openings. Start paragraphs with subjects instead.
- **Uniform paragraph/sentence length** — Human writing is lumpy: short punchy sentences mixed with longer ones. Vary deliberately.
- **Sycophantic openers** — "Great question!", "Excellent point!", "That's a really interesting..." — delete.
- **The false-depth pattern** — Restates the problem in fancier words, lists obvious considerations, concludes vaguely. Replace with specifics, data, edge cases.
- **List symmetry addiction** — Exactly 3 or 5 items, all same grammatical structure. Real lists are uneven.

## Punctuation Tells

- **Semicolon avoidance** — LLMs rarely use semicolons. Add some where appropriate (connecting related independent clauses).
- **Colon overuse in headings** — "Topic: The Subtitle Pattern" repeated. Vary heading styles.
- **Hedging chains** — "can," "may," "might," "could potentially," "it's possible that." State facts or admit uncertainty directly.
